Student Loan Discharge

Borrower Defense to Repayment — the federal program that can cancel your federal student loans and refund what you paid if your school lied to you or broke the law to get you to enroll. Here's whether you qualify, how to prove it, and exactly how to apply.

Educational, not legal advice. Borrower Defense is for borrowers whose school committed misconduct or misrepresentation — not a general "I can't afford my loans" program. Applying is always free, directly at StudentAid.gov.

★What Borrower Defense can do

If your school misled you — about job placement rates, accreditation, transferable credits, costs, or what the program would deliver — federal law lets you ask the U.S. Department of Education to discharge the federal loans you took out for that school, and potentially refund payments you already made.

iThe honest picture in 2026

Read this before you pin hopes on a timeline. The application is open and free, but the program has been through heavy litigation and staffing cuts. Decisions are slow, and the Department can take up to three years to rule on a complete claim. File anyway — your claim is dated when you submit, and a strong, well-documented application is what matters.
  • Applications are open at StudentAid.gov/borrower-defense and remain free.
  • Older claims (filed 2017–2022) are governed by the Sweet v. McMahon class settlement, which set deadlines for the Department to decide many claims.
  • The Department resumed sending claim notices to schools in 2026 for newer claims.
  • Because rules keep shifting, always confirm current status on StudentAid.gov when you apply.

1First: what loans do you have?

Borrower Defense only discharges federal Direct Loans (including Direct PLUS and Direct Consolidation Loans).

  • FFEL or Perkins loans? They don't qualify on their own — but you can consolidate them into a Direct Consolidation Loan first to become eligible. (Note: consolidating can affect other benefits and resets some clocks — weigh it carefully.)
  • Private loans? Borrower Defense does not cover them. Look instead at claims against the lender or school under state law.

A strong claim generally shows all four. Tick the ones that match your experience.

✓Common qualifying misconduct

  • Inflated or fake job-placement or salary rates ("94% of grads hired").
  • Lying about accreditation or that credits would transfer.
  • Misrepresenting cost, financial aid, or loan terms.
  • Promising certification or licensure the program couldn't actually provide.
  • High-pressure or deceptive recruiting tactics; falsified your application or aid forms.
  • The school closed while you were enrolled or right after (may also qualify for a Closed School Discharge).

▤Build your evidence file

You don't need a lawyer's case — just credible proof of the gap between what you were promised and what you got. Check off what you can gather. Saved on this device.

⌕Where to find it

  • Your own records: enrollment agreement, catalog, emails/texts with recruiters, brochures, screenshots of the school's website (try the Wayback Machine for old pages).
  • Public findings: lawsuits or settlements by the FTC, CFPB, or your state attorney general against the school — these strengthen claims a lot.
  • Your loan details: log in to StudentAid.gov to list every Direct Loan tied to that school and the amounts.
  • Your story: a clear, dated, first-person account of what you were told and how you relied on it.

✉Prefer paper?

Download the PDF application from StudentAid.gov and mail it to: U.S. Department of Education, Borrower Defense to Repayment, P.O. Box 1854, Monticello, KY 42633. Keep a full copy.

⏸While you wait

  • Forbearance / stopped collections: you can request that payments pause and collections stop while your claim is pending. Interest may still accrue in forbearance — ask your servicer how it's handled.
  • Keep applying pressure where appropriate: if your school faced government action, reference it.
  • Don't ignore your loans otherwise — a pending claim isn't the same as a discharge until you get a decision.

§The law behind it

Higher Education Act §455(h) · 20 U.S.C. §1087e(h)
The federal statute authorizing borrowers to assert a defense to repayment based on the school's acts or omissions.
34 CFR Part 685, Subpart D
The regulations that define the standard, evidence, and process for Borrower Defense on Direct Loans.
Closed School Discharge · 34 CFR §685.214
A separate path: if your school closed while you were enrolled or shortly after you left, your loans may be cancelled.
False Certification Discharge
If the school falsely certified your eligibility (e.g., signed you up though you couldn't benefit), that's another route to cancellation.
Sweet v. McMahon (settlement)
Governs many 2017–2022 claims and set timelines for the Department to issue decisions. Relevant if you applied in that window.
